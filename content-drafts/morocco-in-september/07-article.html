September sits between Morocco's hottest month and its most temperate one, which is exactly why it draws so many questions. If you're weighing the best places to visit in Morocco in September, the honest answer depends on where in the country you go and which week of the month you land. Marrakech and the interior plains still run warm, especially in the first half of the month, while Essaouira and the Atlantic coast stay noticeably milder throughout. The High Atlas Mountains offer some of the year's best hiking conditions once the summer heat breaks, and the Sahara near Merzouga becomes more comfortable for excursions as September progresses, though midday heat still requires planning. This guide, built from current climate data and on-the-ground travel knowledge at Authentic Morocco Adventures, walks through where to go, what weather to expect, and how to build a realistic itinerary.

Is September a Good Time to Visit Morocco?

September is generally a good time to visit Morocco, particularly for travelers who want to avoid peak summer crowds and don't mind some lingering heat early in the month. Early September can still feel like summer in Marrakech, Fes, and the desert, with daytime highs regularly above 30°C (86°F). By late September, temperatures ease across most of the country, mornings and evenings turn genuinely pleasant, and the High Atlas Mountains enter one of their most reliable hiking windows. Coastal cities like Essaouira stay mild all month thanks to Atlantic breezes, making them a good counterbalance to a hot inland itinerary. September also falls just after Morocco's busiest tourist season, so accommodation and desert camps are somewhat easier to book than in July or August, though popular riads and camps still fill up. Travelers who are heat-sensitive should lean toward the second half of the month or prioritize the coast and mountains over the desert and interior cities.

Morocco Weather in September: What to Expect

Morocco's September weather is transitional and varies significantly by region — hot and dry inland, mild and breezy on the Atlantic coast, and increasingly comfortable at elevation as the month goes on. Treating "Morocco weather in September" as one single climate is the most common planning mistake; the distance and elevation difference between Marrakech and Essaouira, or between Merzouga and the High Atlas, is enough to produce genuinely different experiences on the same day.

Early September versus late September. The first two weeks of September often still carry summer's momentum, especially inland: Marrakech, Fes, and the desert can all see daytime highs in the low-to-mid 30s°C (86–95°F). By the final third of the month, average highs ease by several degrees across most of the country, and the shift is usually most noticeable in the mountains and at night everywhere.

Inland cities (Marrakech, Fes, Ouarzazate). Expect daytime highs commonly in the 29–34°C (84–93°F) range in early-to-mid September, dropping into the high 20s°C by late month. Evenings cool to a comfortable 18–20°C (64–68°F), which makes rooftop dinners and evening souk visits far more pleasant than the midday hours.

Atlantic coast (Essaouira, Taghazout). The Atlantic current keeps this coastline notably cooler than the interior year-round. Essaouira typically sees daytime highs around 22–25°C (72–77°F) in September, with a defining sea breeze that can feel cool in the evening even when inland Morocco is still hot. This makes the coast one of the most reliable September options for travelers who find inland heat uncomfortable.

Sahara Desert (Merzouga, Erg Chebbi). September desert days are still hot, commonly 30–35°C (86–95°F), while nights cool substantially, sometimes into the high teens or low 20s°C. Interestingly, September is statistically one of the wetter months in this part of the Sahara, though rainfall remains infrequent and generally light. The practical implication is the same either way: plan dune excursions, camel treks, and camp arrivals around early morning and late afternoon rather than midday.

High Atlas Mountains. This is where September earns its reputation as a strong shoulder-season month. Daytime temperatures in trekking areas around Imlil and Toubkal typically sit near 20–25°C, with cool nights, especially at higher camps. Conditions are generally stable, but mountain weather can change within hours, and by late September isolated snow patches can begin appearing at the highest elevations. Proper layering is not optional here.

Sun protection and hydration. Regardless of region, September in Morocco still means strong midday sun. Sunscreen, a hat, sunglasses, and consistent water intake matter as much in September as in peak summer, particularly in the desert and inland cities.

Climate averages can help with planning, but they are not a forecast. Check updated local conditions shortly before departure.

1. Marrakech: Culture, Food and Late-Summer Energy

Marrakech in September rewards travelers who plan around the heat rather than against it. The city remains warm, sometimes hot, in the first half of the month, but mornings and evenings become genuinely enjoyable, and by late September daytime temperatures ease into a more comfortable range.

Weather character: Daytime highs often 29–34°C early in the month, easing toward the high 20s°C by late September; evenings around 18–20°C.

Best activities: Jemaa el-Fna at dusk, Marrakech Medina souks, Majorelle Garden, Bahia Palace, a Marrakech food tour through the Medina's street food scene, and day trips into the surrounding countryside once temperatures drop in the afternoon.

Ideal length of stay: 2–3 nights as a base, more if using it as a hub for day trips.

Who it suits: First-time visitors, food and culture travelers, couples, and anyone building a multi-region itinerary around a central hub.

Potential disadvantages: Midday heat in early September can make extended outdoor walking uncomfortable; the Medina's narrow alleys hold heat.

Local tip: Structure Medina and souk visits for morning or after 5 p.m., and reserve midday hours for riad courtyards, museums, or a hammam and Moroccan massage.

Next destination: Marrakech connects naturally to the Agafay Desert for a short desert taste, or onward toward Ouarzazate and Merzouga for a full Sahara route. See the full range of tours departing from Marrakech.

2. Merzouga and Erg Chebbi: Sahara Desert Nights

Merzouga, a small town bordering the Erg Chebbi dunes in southeastern Morocco, is the standard gateway for Sahara Desert experiences. September is a genuine transition month here: hot by day, but with camp evenings that are far more comfortable than in July or August.

Weather character: Daytime highs commonly 30–35°C, dropping into cooler double digits at night; occasional light, localized rain is statistically more likely in September than in peak summer, though still uncommon.

Best activities: Camel trekking into the dunes at sunset, overnight desert camps, 4x4 desert excursions, sandboarding, and stargazing once temperatures drop after dark.

Ideal length of stay: 1 night minimum, 2 nights if time allows for a fuller desert experience.

Who it suits: Adventure travelers, photographers chasing dune light, and anyone building a classic south-of-Morocco route.

Potential disadvantages: Long drive from Marrakech or Fes (typically a full day, often split across two days with stops); midday sun in the dunes is intense even with cooler nights.

Local tip: Book camel or 4x4 excursions for early morning or the two hours before sunset — the light is better for photography and the heat is far more manageable.

Next destination: Most itineraries route back through the Dades Valley and Todra Gorge, or return via Ouarzazate. 3. High Atlas Mountains: Morocco's Best September Trekking

The High Atlas Mountains, rising south of Marrakech toward peaks including Jbel Toubkal, offer some of the most favorable trekking conditions of the year in September. As summer heat breaks, trails around Imlil and the Toubkal region become notably more comfortable than in July or August.

Weather character: Daytime temperatures typically around 20–25°C at trekking elevations, cooling sharply at night; conditions are generally stable but can shift quickly at altitude.

Best activities: Day hikes from Imlil, multi-day Toubkal treks, Berber village visits, and valley walks through the Ourika Valley.

Ideal length of stay: 1 day for a valley visit, 2–4 days for a proper trekking itinerary.

Who it suits: Adventure travelers, hikers, and anyone wanting a cooler counterpoint to Marrakech's heat.

Potential disadvantages: Weather at altitude is less predictable than in the lowlands; by late September, isolated snow patches can appear at the highest elevations, and a local guide is strongly recommended for multi-day routes.

Local tip: Pack proper layers even for a day trip — mountain temperature drops are sharper and faster than most first-time visitors expect.

Next destination: The Ourika Valley day trip from Marrakech pairs naturally with a return to Marrakech. For a dedicated trekking itinerary, see the 2-day Toubkal trekking route, the 3-day Berber villages trek, or the 3-day Toubkal summit trek.

4. Essaouira: The Cool Atlantic Counterpoint

Essaouira, an Atlantic coastal city roughly two and a half hours west of Marrakech, is one of the most reliable September destinations for travelers who want a break from inland heat. Its climate stays mild and breezy nearly year-round due to a cold Atlantic current offshore.

Weather character: Daytime highs typically 22–25°C, evenings cooling into the high teens; steady sea breeze, with wind generally easing somewhat compared to peak summer months.

Best activities: Walking the fortified Medina walls, exploring the port and souks, seafood dining, and watersports such as windsurfing and kitesurfing for wind-comfortable travelers.

Ideal length of stay: 1–2 nights, or a full-day trip from Marrakech.

Who it suits: Travelers sensitive to heat, couples wanting a slower pace, photographers, and anyone pairing a beach day with a cultural itinerary.

Potential disadvantages: Wind can be strong enough to affect beach comfort; fewer major historical sites than Marrakech or Fes.

Local tip: Bring a light jacket even on a warm-looking day — the coastal breeze cools quickly once the sun starts to drop.

Next destination: Essaouira works well as a standalone addition to a Marrakech-based itinerary via the Marrakech to Essaouira day trip, or as a stop on the 2-day Marrakech desert tour to Essaouira.

5. Ouarzazate and Aït Benhaddou: Kasbahs and Film History

Ouarzazate and the nearby Aït Benhaddou, a historic ksar (fortified village) roughly 30 km northwest of Ouarzazate and a UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1987, form one of southern Morocco's most photographed routes. September's drier, slightly cooler conditions make walking the ksar's narrow lanes considerably more comfortable than in summer.

Weather character: Warm and dry, typically high 20s to low 30s°C, with very little rainfall expected.

Best activities: Walking through Aït Benhaddou's ksar, visiting Ouarzazate's film studios, and exploring the Taourirt Kasbah.

Ideal length of stay: Half a day for Aït Benhaddou, 1 night in Ouarzazate if continuing toward the desert.

Who it suits: Photographers, history-focused travelers, and anyone en route between Marrakech and Merzouga.

Potential disadvantages: Little natural shade at Aït Benhaddou; the site can be visited quickly, so it works best as a stop rather than a standalone destination.

Local tip: Visit Aït Benhaddou in the early morning to avoid both the heat and the midday tour-bus crowds.

Next destination: Ouarzazate leads naturally onward to the Dades Valley and Todra Gorge, or south to Merzouga — see the 2-day Ouarzazate to Merzouga route. For travelers short on time, the Marrakech to Aït Benhaddou day trip is a common add-on, or explore all tours departing from Ouarzazate.

6. Dades Valley and Todra Gorge: Canyon Country

The Dades Valley and Todra Gorge, east of Ouarzazate along Morocco's south-facing Atlas foothills, offer dramatic canyon and rock-formation scenery. September's stable, drier weather makes this one of the better months for the drive and short walks through the gorge.

Weather character: Warm days, typically 25–30°C, cooling more noticeably at night than in the desert proper due to elevation.

Best activities: Driving the Dades Valley's switchback roads, walking into Todra Gorge's canyon, and photographing the rock formations at golden hour.

Ideal length of stay: 1 night, primarily as a stop between Ouarzazate and Merzouga.

Who it suits: Road-trip travelers, photographers, and those combining it with a broader south Morocco loop.

Potential disadvantages: This is a driving-heavy region; distances between photogenic stops are longer than they first appear on a map.

Local tip: Time the Todra Gorge walk for late afternoon, when the canyon walls catch warm light and the heat has eased.

Next destination: This region connects directly to Merzouga to the southeast, making it a natural midpoint on the 3-day Sahara Desert tour from Marrakech to Chigaga or similar multi-day routes.

7. Fes: The Cultural Capital's Medina

Fes, home to one of the world's largest car-free urban areas in its historic Medina, remains warm in September but becomes more walkable as the month progresses. Its dense, covered souks offer natural shade that can make midday exploration more bearable than in open-air Marrakech.

Weather character: Daytime highs around 29–33°C early in the month, easing toward late September; nights noticeably cooler.

Best activities: Fes el-Bali Medina walks on a guided Medina tour in Fes, tannery viewpoints, madrasa architecture, and craft workshops (leather, ceramics, metalwork).

Ideal length of stay: 2 nights.

Who it suits: History and craft-focused travelers, and those prioritizing depth over breadth in one city.

Potential disadvantages: The Medina's maze-like layout is demanding in heat and can be disorienting without a guide.

Local tip: Hire a local guide for the first Medina visit — it saves time and adds context that's easy to miss navigating alone.

Next destination: Fes is a natural starting point for desert routes heading toward Merzouga via the Middle Atlas, as an alternative to the Marrakech route — see the 3-day Fes to Merzouga desert tour or browse all tours departing from Fes.

8. Chefchaouen: The Blue City in the Rif Mountains

Chefchaouen, set in the Rif Mountains in northern Morocco, is at its best from mid-September onward, when temperatures ease from summer's peak and the surrounding hills remain green.

Weather character: Mild to warm, generally mid-20s°C by day, cooling into the mid-to-high teens at night; low rain probability.

Best activities: Walking the blue-washed Medina lanes, day hikes to the Spanish Mosque viewpoint, and excursions to the Akchour waterfalls (lower flow than spring, but still worthwhile).

Ideal length of stay: 1–2 nights.

Who it suits: Photographers, slower-paced travelers, and those extending a northern Morocco route beyond Fes.

Potential disadvantages: Farther from Marrakech than most other destinations on this list, typically requiring a flight into a northern airport or a long overland connection; better suited to a dedicated northern itinerary than a quick add-on.

Local tip: Visit in the early morning for the best light on the blue walls and before the town's narrow streets fill with day-trippers.

Next destination: Chefchaouen pairs most naturally with Fes on the 2-day Fes to Chefchaouen route or the Fes to Chefchaouen day trip, rather than the classic south-desert circuit.

9. Agafay Desert: A Short Desert Escape Near Marrakech

The Agafay Desert, a rocky desert landscape roughly 30–40 minutes from Marrakech, offers a desert-style experience without the long drive to Merzouga. It suits travelers who want dunes-adjacent scenery within a tight schedule.

Weather character: Hot by day, similar to Marrakech, but evenings cool down pleasantly, making it well suited to late-afternoon-into-evening visits.

Best activities: Sunset camel rides, quad biking, dinner under the stars at a desert camp, and a Marrakech hot air balloon flight at dawn over the surrounding landscape.

Ideal length of stay: Half a day to one overnight camp stay.

Who it suits: Travelers with limited time, families wanting a taste of desert scenery without a long drive, and those combining it with a short Marrakech stay.

Potential disadvantages: It is rocky terrain rather than the classic sand dunes of Erg Chebbi; not a substitute for a full Sahara experience.

Local tip: Go for sunset rather than midday — the temperature drop and light change make the experience considerably more comfortable and photogenic.

Next destination: Agafay is typically a standalone half-day or overnight addition to a Marrakech stay. Book directly via the Agafay Desert luxury camp experience or the Agafay Desert tour from Marrakech.

10. Casablanca and Rabat: A Practical Coastal Add-On

For travelers arriving or departing via Casablanca, pairing it with a short stop in Rabat is a practical way to bookend a September Morocco trip without adding significant driving time.

Weather character: Mild and coastal, similar in character to Essaouira though generally slightly warmer, with steady sea breeze moderating the heat.

Best activities: Casablanca's Hassan II Mosque area, coastal corniche walks, and Rabat's historic sites on a Casablanca to Rabat day trip.

Ideal length of stay: 1 night in Casablanca, or a single day if using it purely as an arrival/departure point.

Who it suits: Travelers with international flights through Casablanca who want to see more than just the airport, and those short on time.

Potential disadvantages: Casablanca is more of a modern business city than a classic Moroccan tourist destination; most travelers use it as a transit point rather than a primary stop.

What to Pack for Morocco in September

  • Lightweight, breathable clothing — natural fabrics like cotton and linen handle inland heat better than synthetics
  • Modest, respectful clothing for Medinas and rural areas — shoulders and knees covered is a practical default, particularly at religious sites
  • Comfortable walking shoes with good grip for uneven Medina cobblestones and desert terrain
  • Sun protection — hat, sunglasses, and high-SPF sunscreen for both city and desert days
  • A refillable water bottle — hydration matters as much in September as in peak summer
  • A light evening layer — even hot inland cities cool down noticeably after sunset
  • Proper mountain layers if trekking in the High Atlas — temperatures drop fast at elevation
  • A warmer layer for desert nights — Sahara evenings can be markedly cooler than daytime highs

  • A small day bag for excursions, markets, and day trips
  • Travel documents — passport, visa information if applicable, travel insurance details, accommodation confirmations
  • Medication and personal essentials, including any prescription medication in original packaging

Practical Tips for Visiting Morocco in September

Book accommodation and desert camps ahead of time. September follows Morocco's busiest tourist months, and while availability is generally better than in summer, well-reviewed riads and desert camps still fill up, particularly around any local festival dates.

Plan outdoor activities for early morning or late afternoon. This applies to Medina walking, desert excursions, and kasbah visits alike — the hours around midday are consistently the hottest across most of the country in September.

Stay ahead of hydration, especially in Marrakech, Fes, and the desert, where dry heat can be deceptive.

Break up long road journeys. Routes like Marrakech–Merzouga involve genuine multi-hour driving days; building in stops (Aït Benhaddou, Dades Valley) makes the trip more comfortable than rushing straight through.

Consider a local guide for Medinas in Marrakech and Fes, and for High Atlas trekking routes — both add context and reduce the disorientation risk of navigating alone.

Carry a mix of cash and cards. Cash is still preferred in souks, smaller towns, and some desert camps; cards work in most hotels and larger establishments.

Be aware of Friday considerations. Some smaller shops and local businesses may close or operate reduced hours around Friday midday prayers, particularly outside major tourist zones.

Photograph people respectfully. Ask before photographing individuals, particularly in rural areas and traditional markets.

Get travel insurance that covers your planned activities, especially if trekking or joining desert excursions.

Check current conditions before departure, since weather, road conditions, and event dates can shift year to year.

Confirm festival dates locally, as several Moroccan cultural and religious events shift annually and should be verified close to your travel dates rather than assumed from prior years.

Avoid overly packed itineraries. September's driving distances between the Atlas Mountains, the desert, and the coast are real; leaving buffer time reduces stress more than adding one more stop does.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is September a good month to visit Morocco?
Yes, September is generally a good month to visit Morocco, especially for travelers avoiding peak summer crowds. Early September can still be hot, particularly inland and in the desert, while late September tends to bring more comfortable temperatures across most regions. Coastal areas like Essaouira remain mild throughout the month.

Is Morocco still hot in September?
Yes, especially early in the month. Inland cities like Marrakech and Fes commonly see highs in the low-to-mid 30s°C (86–95°F) in early September, easing somewhat by late month. The Atlantic coast stays considerably cooler throughout, so heat-sensitive travelers can moderate their experience by choosing coastal stops.

Is Marrakech too hot in September?
Not necessarily, but midday heat in early September can be uncomfortable for extended outdoor walking. Mornings and evenings are pleasant throughout the month, and by late September, daytime temperatures become noticeably more manageable. Planning Medina and souk visits around the cooler parts of the day is the practical solution.

Can you visit the Sahara Desert in September?
Yes. September is a viable month for Merzouga and Erg Chebbi, though days remain hot (commonly 30–35°C) while nights are cooler and more comfortable. Camel treks and 4x4 excursions are best scheduled for early morning or late afternoon to avoid the most intense midday heat.

What should I wear in Morocco in September?
Lightweight, breathable clothing for daytime heat, a light layer for cooler evenings, and modest coverage (shoulders and knees) for Medinas and rural areas. Add proper layers for High Atlas trekking and a warmer layer for desert nights, where temperatures drop more than daytime heat suggests.

Is September crowded in Morocco?
September is less crowded than July and August, Morocco's peak tourist months, but it is not a quiet off-season. Accommodation and desert camps are generally easier to book than in summer, though popular options can still fill up, particularly around local festival periods.

Can you swim in Morocco in September?
Yes, along the Atlantic coast, though water temperatures are cool rather than warm — around 21°C (70°F) near Essaouira. Riad and hotel pools inland offer a warmer alternative, and are a common way to cool off after a hot day in Marrakech or Fes.

Is September good for hiking in the Atlas Mountains?
Yes, September is one of the better months for High Atlas trekking, as summer heat eases and conditions generally stabilize. Daytime temperatures at trekking elevations are typically comfortable, though weather can shift quickly, and by late September isolated snow can appear at the highest elevations.

How many days should I spend in Morocco?
Seven to ten days allows a realistic combination of Marrakech, the kasbah route, and the Sahara Desert without rushing the driving days involved. Five days works for a Marrakech-centered trip with shorter excursions, while two weeks allows an additional northern Morocco leg (Fes, Chefchaouen).

What is the best Morocco itinerary for September?
The best itinerary depends on available time and heat tolerance, but a 7 to 10-day route combining Marrakech, Aït Benhaddou, the Dades Valley or Todra Gorge, and a Merzouga desert camp covers Morocco's most requested experiences. Adding Essaouira balances the trip with a cooler coastal stop.

Does it rain in Morocco in September?
Rain is possible but generally light and infrequent. Interestingly, September is statistically one of the wetter months in the Moroccan Sahara compared to peak summer, though rainfall there remains uncommon overall. Coastal and inland cities typically see minimal rainfall in September as well.

Should I book a private Morocco tour in advance?
Booking ahead is advisable, particularly for desert camps, popular riads, and any dates overlapping with local festivals. A private tour also simplifies the long driving segments between regions, which is one of the more demanding logistical aspects of a September Morocco itinerary.
